SOCIAL STUDIES: Meet the Three Fires Confederacy

Video: The Three Fires Confederacy
Read the following passage carefully, then answer the comprehension questions.

Long before any European ship crossed the Atlantic Ocean, the Great Lakes region was home to thriving Indigenous nations. Among the most powerful was an alliance called the Three Fires Confederacy, made up of three related Anishinaabe peoples: the Ojibwe, the Odawa, and the Potawatomi. These three nations shared similar languages, beliefs, and customs, but each had their own special role in the alliance.

The Ojibwe, sometimes called Chippewa, were known as the "Keepers of the Faith." They were skilled hunters and gatherers who developed deep knowledge of the forests and waterways.

The Odawa, also spelled Ottawa, earned the title "Keepers of Trade." They were master negotiators and traders who traveled vast distances by canoe, creating trade networks that stretched hundreds of miles. The Odawa traded copper, furs, corn, and other goods between different nations. Their trading skills would later make them important partners—and sometimes rivals—of European fur traders.

The Potawatomi were called the "Keepers of the Fire," which was a sacred responsibility. Fire represented the heart of the community—the place where people gathered, where food was prepared, and where important decisions were made. The Potawatomi lived in the southern Great Lakes region and were known for their farming skills, growing corn, beans, and squash in the rich soil.

Together, these three nations created a powerful alliance based on cooperation rather than competition. When one nation faced a threat, the others came to help. When one nation had extra food, they shared with the others. This system worked for thousands of years before Europeans arrived and began to change everything.

MATH: The Area Model for Division

Video: Area Model Division
The Area Model

The area model breaks division into friendly chunks. For 84 / 7:

Step 1: Find a friendly multiple of 7 that fits inside 84

  • 7 x 10 = 70 (that fits!)
Step 2: Subtract and see what's left
  • 84 - 70 = 14
Step 3: Divide what's left
  • 14 / 7 = 2
Step 4: Add your partial quotients
  • 10 + 2 = 12
So 84 / 7 = 12

SOCIAL STUDIES: Anishinaabe Life Before Contact

Read the following passage carefully, then answer the comprehension questions.

Many old history books described Indigenous peoples as "primitive" or "uncivilized." This was wrong, and it was often written by Europeans who didn't understand—or didn't want to understand—what they were seeing. The Anishinaabe and other Indigenous nations had built complex, sophisticated societies that had thrived for thousands of years before Europeans arrived.

Anishinaabe communities were governed by councils made up of respected leaders, elders, and clan mothers. Unlike European kings who inherited power, Anishinaabe leaders earned their positions through wisdom, generosity, and the ability to build consensus. A leader who became selfish or made bad decisions could be removed. Decisions were made through discussion and agreement, not by one person giving orders.

The Anishinaabe economy was based on a deep understanding of the natural world. They knew exactly when to plant corn, when the fish would run, when berries would ripen, and where to find medicinal plants. They practiced sustainable harvesting—taking only what they needed and ensuring there would be plenty for future generations. Their trade networks stretched for thousands of miles, connecting nations from the Atlantic coast to the Great Plains.

One of the biggest differences between Anishinaabe and European societies was the role of women. In most European countries at this time, women could not own property, vote, or hold leadership positions. But in Anishinaabe society, women held real power. Women owned the homes and gardens. Clan membership—which determined your family, your responsibilities, and your place in society—passed through the mother, not the father. Clan mothers helped choose leaders and could remove them if they failed the community. Women controlled food distribution, which was one of the most important forms of power in any society.

The Anishinaabe also had rich intellectual traditions. Elders passed down history, science, and moral teachings through stories and ceremonies. They had detailed knowledge of astronomy, using the stars for navigation and to track seasons. Their medicine included hundreds of plant-based treatments, many of which modern scientists have confirmed actually work.

SCIENCE: Energy and Motion (Q1 Review)

Video: Bill Nye: Energy
Read the following passage carefully, then answer the comprehension questions.

Energy is everywhere, even when you can't see it. Scientists define energy as the ability to do work or cause change. When you throw a ball, lift a book, or even think a thought, energy is involved. One of the most important things to understand about energy is that it cannot be created or destroyed—it can only change forms or transfer from one object to another.

There are two main categories of energy: kinetic energy and potential energy. Kinetic energy is the energy of motion. Anything that is moving has kinetic energy—a running athlete, a flying baseball, a rolling skateboard, even the tiny molecules in the air around you. The faster something moves, the more kinetic energy it has. A baseball thrown at 90 miles per hour has much more kinetic energy than one thrown at 50 miles per hour.

Potential energy is stored energy—energy that is waiting to be used. When you hold a ball above your head, it has potential energy because of its position. The higher you hold it, the more potential energy it has. When you let go, that potential energy transforms into kinetic energy as the ball falls. A stretched rubber band has potential energy too—when you release it, that stored energy becomes motion.

Energy transfer happens constantly in our world. Think about what happens when a baseball bat hits a ball. The batter's muscles convert chemical energy (from food) into kinetic energy as they swing. The moving bat has kinetic energy. When the bat contacts the ball, energy transfers from the bat to the ball, sending it flying. But not all the energy goes into the ball's motion. Some energy transforms into sound (the crack of the bat), some becomes heat from friction, and some causes vibrations in the bat and ball. This is why your hands might sting after hitting a ball—energy transferred into your body!

Understanding energy helps us understand how everything in the world works, from how cars move to why we need to eat food to how the sun keeps our planet warm.

Reading Comprehension

1. What is the scientific definition of energy?

4.PS3.A

2. Explain the difference between kinetic energy and potential energy. Give one example of each.

4.PS3.A

3. According to the passage, what happens to energy when a bat hits a ball? List at least THREE forms the energy takes.

4.PS3.A

4. The passage says energy "cannot be created or destroyed." What happens to energy instead?

4.PS3.A

SOCIAL STUDIES: The Fur Trade Changes Everything

Video: The Fur Trade Explained
Read the following passage carefully, then answer the comprehension questions.

In the 1600s, something strange was happening in Europe: everyone wanted beaver fur hats. Wealthy men considered beaver hats a sign of status and fashion, and the demand was enormous. There was just one problem—Europeans had hunted beavers nearly to extinction in their own countries. When French explorers reached the Great Lakes region and discovered millions of beavers, they saw an opportunity to make fortunes.

But the French faced a challenge. They didn't know the land. They didn't know where to find beavers or how to trap them efficiently. They didn't know how to survive the harsh winters or navigate the complex waterways. The Indigenous peoples, however, had this knowledge. The Anishinaabe and other nations had been trapping beavers and preparing furs for generations. They knew the forests, rivers, and seasons better than any European ever could.

This created what seemed like a fair exchange. Indigenous trappers would hunt beavers and prepare the furs using traditional methods. In return, the French would provide European trade goods: metal knives that held their edge longer than stone, iron cooking pots, wool blankets, glass beads, and—most significantly—guns and ammunition. At first, both sides benefited. The French got valuable furs to ship back to Europe. Indigenous peoples got useful new tools that made daily life easier.

But the trade relationship gradually shifted. As Indigenous peoples began relying on European goods, they needed to trap more and more beavers to trade. Traditional hunting practices, which had focused on taking only what was needed, gave way to intensive trapping to meet European demand. Some areas were hunted out completely. Meanwhile, Indigenous peoples who had grown accustomed to metal tools and guns found it difficult to return to traditional methods.

The French understood this power shift and used it. If a nation refused to cooperate with French demands, traders could simply stop providing goods that people had come to depend on. No more gunpowder meant hunters couldn't defend themselves against enemies who still had guns. No more metal tools meant harder daily work. What started as an equal partnership became increasingly unequal, with Europeans holding more and more power.

4-H3.0.5

MATH: Division with Remainders

Video: Understanding Remainders
What is a Remainder?

Sometimes numbers don't divide evenly. The "leftover" is called the remainder.

Example: 17 / 5 = 3 R2

  • 5 goes into 17 three times (5 x 3 = 15)
  • 17 - 15 = 2 left over
  • We write this as 3 R2 or 3 remainder 2
Real life: If 17 players need to form teams of 5, you get 3 complete teams with 2 players left over.

Remainder Practice

1. 23 / 4 = ?

4.NBT.6
5 R3 (4 x 5 = 20, 23 - 20 = 3)

2. 50 / 7 = ?

4.NBT.6
7 R1 (7 x 7 = 49, 50 - 49 = 1)

3. The team has 47 balls to put in bags of 6. How many full bags? How many balls left over?

4.NBT.6
7 full bags, 5 balls left over (6 x 7 = 42, 47 - 42 = 5)

Friction happens when two surfaces rub against each other. It converts kinetic energy into heat.

MATH: What Do You Do With the Remainder?

Remainders in Real Life: Three Choices

Depending on the situation, you might:

1. Drop it - "How many complete teams of 6 from 20 players?" Answer: 3 (ignore the 2 extras)

2. Round up - "How many vans for 23 people if each van holds 8?" Answer: 3 vans (you need that extra van for the remaining 7 people!)

SOCIAL STUDIES: European Powers Compete for Control

Read the following passage carefully, then answer the comprehension questions.

The French were not the only Europeans interested in the Great Lakes region. The British, who had colonies along the Atlantic coast, wanted access to the valuable fur trade too. By the mid-1700s, France and Britain were locked in a fierce competition for control of North America. This competition would have enormous consequences for Indigenous peoples, who found themselves caught in the middle.

At first, many Indigenous nations preferred dealing with the French. French traders often lived among Indigenous communities, learned their languages, and married Indigenous women. When Britain won, France was forced to give up its claims to Canada and the Great Lakes region. This was devastating news for Indigenous peoples who had allied with France. Their French partners were gone, and now they had to deal with the British—who had very different ideas about how to treat Indigenous nations.

The British government issued the Proclamation of 1763, which said colonists could not settle west of the Appalachian Mountains. This was supposed to protect Indigenous lands. But British colonists ignored the proclamation and kept pushing westward anyway, setting the stage for more conflict.

SOCIAL STUDIES: Treaties - Promises Made, Promises Broken

Read the following passage carefully, then answer the comprehension questions.

Treaties were formal agreements between Indigenous nations and European or American governments. In theory, they were nation-to-nation agreements between equals, similar to treaties between France and England or Spain and Portugal. Indigenous leaders understood treaties as sacred promises that bound both parties. When they signed, they believed they were entering a relationship of mutual respect and obligation.

But European and American governments often saw treaties very differently. For them, treaties were tools to gain land as cheaply as possible while avoiding costly wars. Government officials knew that fighting Indigenous nations was expensive and dangerous—it was much easier to get land through negotiation, even if those negotiations weren't entirely honest.

The treaty process itself was often unfair. Treaties were written in English, which many Indigenous leaders could not read. Government translators sometimes deliberately changed the meaning of important phrases. Indigenous peoples might agree to "share" land, only to discover the treaty said they had "sold" it permanently. Different cultural ideas about land ownership caused confusion too. Many Indigenous peoples believed land could not be owned—it belonged to everyone, like air or water. When they agreed to let settlers use land, they didn't understand they were giving up all rights to it forever.

The Treaty of Detroit (1807) is one painful example from Michigan's history. In this treaty, Anishinaabe nations agreed to give up millions of acres in southeastern Michigan. In exchange, the U.S. government promised yearly payments, guaranteed hunting and fishing rights, and pledged to protect remaining Indigenous lands from white settlers. Within just a few years, the government had broken almost every promise. Payments arrived late or not at all. Settlers flooded onto "protected" lands. Hunting and fishing rights were ignored.

The most devastating fact is that this was not unusual. Historians estimate that the United States broke almost every single treaty it signed with Indigenous nations. Promises of "permanent" land boundaries lasted only until white settlers wanted that land. Payment promises were forgotten when they became inconvenient. This pattern of broken promises is one of the most shameful chapters in American history.

SCIENCE: Air Resistance in Action

Read the following passage carefully, then answer the comprehension questions.

You can't see air, but you can certainly feel it when you're moving fast. Stick your hand out the window of a moving car, and you'll feel the air pushing against you. This push is called air resistance, and it affects everything that moves through the atmosphere. Understanding air resistance helps explain everything from why baseballs curve to why airplanes can fly.

Air resistance is a type of friction—but instead of two solid surfaces rubbing against each other, it's an object rubbing against the tiny molecules in the air. The faster an object moves, the more air molecules it hits every second, which means more resistance. A baseball thrown at 90 miles per hour experiences much more air resistance than one thrown at 50 miles per hour.

The shape of an object dramatically affects how much air resistance it experiences. Streamlined shapes—like the pointed nose of a jet or the curved body of a dolphin—slice through the air easily. Flat or irregular shapes push against more air and slow down faster. This is why race cars are designed to be low and sleek. It's also why cyclists crouch down over their handlebars—they're making themselves more streamlined to reduce air resistance.

Surface area matters too. A parachute works because it has a huge surface area that catches a lot of air, creating massive resistance that slows a falling person down to a safe speed. A crumpled piece of paper falls faster than a flat sheet because the flat sheet has more surface area pushing against the air. Baseball pitchers use these principles to throw tricky pitches. A curveball spins in a way that makes air push harder on one side of the ball than the other, causing it to curve as it flies toward the batter.

4.PS3.B

3. The passage gives several examples of how shape affects air resistance. Describe TWO of these examples.

SOCIAL STUDIES: Indigenous Women's Power

Read the following passage carefully, then answer the comprehension questions.

When European men first encountered Anishinaabe and other Indigenous societies, they often completely misunderstood what they were seeing. Coming from cultures where women had few legal rights, they assumed Indigenous women must be oppressed too. Some early European writers described Indigenous women as "slaves" to their husbands because they saw women doing agricultural work. What these observers failed to understand was that in Indigenous societies, controlling food production was a source of power, not a sign of low status.

In Anishinaabe society, women held significant authority that European women of the same era could only dream of. Women owned property—including the homes their families lived in and the gardens they cultivated. When a couple married, the husband typically moved into the wife's family home, not the other way around. If a marriage ended, the house and its contents belonged to the woman. In Europe, married women legally could not own property at all; everything belonged to their husbands.

Political power flowed through women in important ways. Anishinaabe society was organized by clans, and a person's clan—which determined their responsibilities, their marriage options, and their place in the community—came from their mother, not their father. Clan mothers were respected elders who helped choose leaders and could remove them if they failed the community. Women also participated in councils that made important decisions about war, peace, and community matters.

Food distribution was another crucial source of women's power. Women controlled how food was stored and shared within the community. In any society, the person who controls food has real power—they decide who eats well and who goes hungry. This wasn't seen as "women's work" in a demeaning sense; it was recognized as vital responsibility that required wisdom and fairness.

European observers often missed these forms of power because they didn't look like European power. European power was visible: crowns, thrones, armies, land deeds with official seals. Indigenous women's power was embedded in family structures, social customs, and daily practices. It was no less real, but Europeans—especially European men—often couldn't or wouldn't see it.

Reading Comprehension

1. Why did early European writers sometimes describe Indigenous women as "slaves"? What did they misunderstand?

4-H3.0.2
They saw women doing agricultural work and assumed this meant low status. They misunderstood that controlling food production was actually a source of power, not oppression.

2. Explain what happened when an Anishinaabe couple got married. How was this different from European marriage customs?

4-H3.0.2
The husband moved into the wife's family home. The house belonged to the woman, and if the marriage ended, she kept it. In Europe, married women couldn't own property—everything belonged to their husbands.

3. What were "clan mothers" and what power did they have?

4-H3.0.2
Clan mothers were respected elders who helped choose leaders and could remove them if they failed the community. They participated in councils making important decisions about war, peace, and community matters.

4. The passage says Europeans "couldn't or wouldn't see" Indigenous women's power. What's the difference between "couldn't" and "wouldn't" in this context?

4-H3.0.2
"Couldn't" means they genuinely didn't understand because the power looked different from what they expected (no crowns, thrones, etc.). "Wouldn't" suggests they chose to ignore women's power because acknowledging it would challenge their belief that European ways were superior.
